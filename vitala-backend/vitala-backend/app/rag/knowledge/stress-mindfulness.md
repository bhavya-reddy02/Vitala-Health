# Stress and mindfulness

Some stress is a normal part of life and can even be helpful in short bursts. Problems arise when stress is intense or ongoing, which can affect sleep, mood, concentration, and physical health. Building simple habits to manage stress protects overall wellbeing.

## Everyday ways to lower stress
Regular physical activity, good sleep, and staying connected to other people are among the most effective and well-established supports for mental wellbeing. Taking short breaks during the day, spending time outdoors, and making time for activities you enjoy all help the nervous system settle.

## Breathing and grounding
Slow breathing can calm the body's stress response. A simple approach is to breathe in gently for a count of about four and out for a count of about six, repeating for a minute or two. Grounding techniques — noticing what you can see, hear, and feel around you — can help when the mind feels overwhelmed or racing.

## Mindfulness in daily life
Mindfulness means paying attention to the present moment with a kind, non-judgemental attitude. It does not require long sessions: pausing to notice your breath, savouring a drink, or doing one task at a time are all small forms of practice. Noting one thing you are grateful for each day is another simple habit that many people find lifts mood over time.

## Balancing demands
Breaking large tasks into smaller steps, setting realistic expectations, and allowing yourself rest can reduce the feeling of being overwhelmed. It is okay to say no and to ask for help.

## When to reach out
If stress, low mood, or anxiety feel persistent, overwhelming, or are affecting daily life, talking to a health professional is a strong and sensible step. If someone is having thoughts of harming themselves, they should seek help urgently from emergency services or a crisis line straight away.

This is general wellbeing information, not medical advice.
