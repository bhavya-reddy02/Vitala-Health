# Hydration and fluids

Staying well hydrated supports energy, concentration, temperature control, and healthy digestion. Water makes up a large part of the body and is lost throughout the day through breathing, sweating, and going to the toilet, so it needs topping up.

## How much to drink
Fluid needs vary a lot from person to person and day to day. They depend on body size, activity, the weather, and overall health. Rather than fixating on a single number, most people can use simple signals: drinking regularly across the day, having a drink with each meal, and drinking more when it is hot or when exercising. Thirst is a helpful early cue — try not to wait until you feel very thirsty.

## A simple self-check
The colour of your urine is a rough, practical guide. Pale straw or light yellow usually suggests you are reasonably hydrated, while darker colour can be a sign to drink more. This is only a general indicator and can be affected by some foods, supplements, and medicines.

## Practical tips
Keep a water bottle within reach, drink a glass when you wake up, and pair drinking with routines you already have such as meals or breaks. Water is the best everyday choice. Drinks with a lot of added sugar are best kept as occasional treats. Tea and coffee also contribute to fluid intake, though a lot of caffeine late in the day can disturb sleep.

## When to seek advice
Feeling constantly thirsty, needing to urinate very often, or dizziness and confusion can have medical causes and are worth discussing with a health professional. People with certain heart, kidney, or other conditions may be advised to limit or adjust their fluids, so personalised guidance from their own clinician always takes priority.

This is general wellbeing information, not medical advice.
