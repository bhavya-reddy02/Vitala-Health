# Sleep and rest

Good sleep is foundational for health. It supports memory, mood, immune function, appetite regulation, and the ability to concentrate and cope with stress. Sleep quality matters as much as quantity.

## How much sleep
Most adults feel and function best with roughly 7 to 9 hours of sleep a night, though the right amount varies between individuals. A useful sign is how you feel during the day: waking reasonably refreshed and staying alert suggests you are getting enough for you.

## Building a healthy sleep routine
A consistent schedule helps the body's internal clock. Going to bed and waking at similar times, including at weekends where possible, makes sleep more reliable. A calm wind-down in the last half hour to hour before bed — dimming lights, stepping away from screens, and doing something relaxing — signals the body that it is time to rest.

## Common disruptors
Caffeine can stay in the body for many hours, so drinks like coffee, strong tea, and energy drinks are best avoided later in the day. Large meals, alcohol, and bright screens close to bedtime can also make sleep lighter or harder to fall into. A cool, dark, quiet bedroom tends to support deeper sleep.

## Daytime habits that help
Getting natural daylight during the day, being physically active, and limiting long naps can all improve night-time sleep. If worries keep you awake, jotting them down earlier in the evening or trying a simple breathing or relaxation exercise can help settle the mind.

## When to seek help
Ongoing trouble falling or staying asleep, loud snoring with pauses in breathing, or persistent tiredness despite enough time in bed are worth discussing with a health professional, as they can have treatable causes.

This is general wellbeing information, not medical advice.
