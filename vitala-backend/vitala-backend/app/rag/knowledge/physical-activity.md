# Physical activity and movement

Regular movement is one of the most powerful things people can do for both physical and mental wellbeing. It supports heart and lung health, strength, balance, mood, and sleep, and helps manage weight and long-term health risks.

## General weekly pattern
A widely used general guide for adults is to aim for around 150 minutes of moderate activity each week — such as brisk walking, cycling, or anything that raises the heart rate and breathing a little — or a smaller amount of more vigorous activity. Alongside this, activities that build muscle strength on two or more days a week are valuable, as is reducing long unbroken periods of sitting. These are general targets to build toward gradually, not strict rules.

## Moderate versus vigorous
Moderate activity makes you breathe faster and feel warmer, but you can still talk. Vigorous activity makes you breathe hard and fast, and talking becomes harder. Both count, and everyday movement like walking, gardening, housework, and taking the stairs all add up.

## Starting out and progressing
If you are new to exercise or returning after a break, start small and increase gradually. Short walks, gentle stretching, and breaking up sitting are excellent first steps. Consistency matters more than intensity at the beginning. Building movement into things you already do — a walk after lunch, stairs instead of the lift — makes it easier to keep up.

## Staying safe
Warm up, wear suitable footwear, stay hydrated, and listen to your body. Some discomfort when building fitness is normal, but sharp pain, chest pain, severe breathlessness, or dizziness are reasons to stop and seek medical advice. Anyone with a health condition, who is pregnant, or who has been inactive for a long time may benefit from checking with a health professional before starting something more intense.

This is general wellbeing information, not medical advice.
